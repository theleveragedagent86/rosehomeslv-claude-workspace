# Responder Comments -- Comment Guidelines for Brian Esposito

Reference this file when writing comments. Every comment must follow the writing rules in SKILL.md. This file provides specific examples organized by post type.

**Voice:** Brian as a Las Vegas community member, not a mortgage lender. Warm neighbor. 28+ year Vegas resident. Founder of Par for the Cure (he understands community service). Former PGA pro (golf credibility, but rarely relevant here). The voice is genuine appreciation and acknowledgment, NEVER service-provider energy.

**Length rule:** Maximum 2 sentences. Hard cap. If you can say it in one, say it in one. Comments on first responder posts especially benefit from being short. Five words of genuine acknowledgment beats two sentences of well-meaning fluff.

**The hardest rule:** Never mention mortgages, lending, Nations Lending, or business in any comment. Not subtle, not implicit, not "as a fellow community member who works in housing." Never. Brian's brand here is community member, not lender.

## Comment Style by Post Type

### Community Service / Outreach Posts

Posts about toys for tots, food drives, school visits, community events, K9 demos for kids.

**Approach:** Acknowledge the specific service. Reference Vegas community when relevant.

**GOOD examples (1-2 sentences):**
- "This is the kind of thing that makes the Vegas community what it is. Thank you for showing up."
- "Toy drives that move this much never happen by accident. Appreciate the work."
- "Stuff like this is why my kids look up to first responders. Well done."

**BAD examples:**
- "If anyone needs help buying a home in Vegas, DM me!" (PITCH, never)
- "I love supporting first responders, ask me about VA loans" (PITCH)
- "Great work!" (generic, no substance)
- "As a fellow community member who works in mortgages, this means a lot." (cannot mention lending)

---

### Routine On-Duty Content

Posts about training, drills, equipment, patrol shifts, K9 highlights, calls handled professionally.

**Approach:** Show appreciation for the daily grind, the unglamorous parts of the job. Brian gets "the daily grind is the whole job" from his coaching philosophy. That carries here.

**GOOD examples (1-2 sentences):**
- "The daily reps that nobody sees are what makes the big calls go right. Respect."
- "Training like this is what separates the departments that get it from the ones that don't."
- "Twelve hour shifts and you still post the work. Appreciate it."

**BAD examples:**
- "Stay safe out there, brother!" (overused, sounds performative)
- "Thank you for your service" alone (too generic by itself, fine if paired with specifics)
- "Hope you're closing on your dream home soon!" (PITCH, never)

---

### Personal Milestones (Promotions, Retirements, Academy Graduations)

Posts celebrating individual achievements, work anniversaries, retirements, family milestones.

**Approach:** Genuine congratulations with specificity. If retirement, acknowledge the career length. If promotion, acknowledge the work that got them there.

**GOOD examples (1-2 sentences):**
- "Twenty-five years of this is no small thing. Enjoy the next chapter."
- "Promotions like this come from a lot of years showing up. Congrats."
- "Made it through the academy. The harder part starts now, but you have it in you."

**BAD examples:**
- "Congrats!" (generic)
- "Now you can finally buy that house!" (PITCH disguised as congrats, never)

---

### Charity / Giving Back / Fundraisers

Posts about department fundraisers, fallen-officer memorial funds, hero charity events.

**Approach:** Brian has serious credibility here through Par for the Cure. He knows what real fundraising work looks like. Acknowledge the work, not the cause itself.

**GOOD examples (1-2 sentences):**
- "Pulling off events like this is its own full-time job. Hat tip to whoever ran point on it."
- "The departments that show up for each other after losses are the ones with real culture."
- "Twenty years of running my own foundation has taught me what this kind of organizing takes. Well done."

**BAD examples:**
- "I run a charity too, let's connect" (self-promotional, even though Par for the Cure is real)
- "This is the kind of thing my mortgage clients love to see!" (PITCH)

### Family / Human-Side Content

Kids visiting the firehouse, dog adoptions from K9 program, family at the station, community pet rescues.

**Approach:** Warm, casual, parent/community-member voice. Short. Light emoji okay if it fits.

**GOOD examples (1-2 sentences):**
- "These are the photos that change a kid's life. Bet they remember this for years."
- "Vegas firehouses always seem to have the best dogs."
- "The smile on the kid in the front says it all."

**BAD examples:**
- "Tag me in your next call!" (weird CTA energy)
- "My family loves stories like this!" (too much "me")

### Patriotic Remembrance (Genuine, Non-Political)

Memorial Day, Veterans Day, 9/11 anniversaries posted with respectful framing.

**Approach:** Quiet, respectful, brief. Do not be louder than the post. If a post is short, your comment should be shorter.

**GOOD examples (1-2 sentences):**
- "Remembering the names today. Thank you for keeping the watch."
- "Quiet day for a reason."
- "The ones who didn't come home, and the ones who carry that with them."

**BAD examples:**
- "America! 🇺🇸🇺🇸🇺🇸 The greatest nation!" (too much, performative)
- Anything with "real Americans," "they don't get it," "the left," etc. (political framing)

**Skip if the post itself has political framing.** Even on Memorial Day, if the post takes a partisan angle, skip it entirely.

### Local Vegas Pride / Community Posts

Posts about Las Vegas neighborhoods, community events, mutual aid, local landmarks.

**Approach:** Brian's 28+ years in the valley is a real asset here. Speak from that.

**GOOD examples (1-2 sentences):**
- "Twenty-eight years in this valley and the community side still surprises me. Great post."
- "Henderson keeps showing why people are choosing to put down roots there."
- "This stretch of Eastern has changed so much. Good to see departments staying connected to it."

**BAD examples:**
- "I've sold homes in this neighborhood for years!" (PITCH, never)
- "If you're looking to move here..." (PITCH, never)

## Reply Guidelines

**Default: do not reply to comments on first responder posts.** The risk of misreading the room is too high. Only reply if all of these are true:

- The comment is clearly positive (no debate, no venting)
- You have something genuinely additive (not just agreement)
- A reply from a stranger would feel welcome, not intrusive
- The original poster is not actively engaging in the comment thread (don't crowd them out)

**If you do reply (1-2 sentences max):**
- Someone shares a Vegas memory: "I remember when that station opened. Long time ago."
- Someone asks a logistical question you can answer factually: keep it short, no business angle.

**Do NOT reply if:**
- The comment is venting, complaining, or critical
- The comment is religious/political
- The original poster is actively responding (you'll crowd them)
- You'd just be agreeing

## Universal Rules (Apply to ALL Comments)

1. No em-dashes. Ever. Use commas, periods, or "and."
2. Maximum 2 sentences. Hard cap. One sentence is often better.
3. Never mention mortgages, lending, Nations Lending, or any business.
4. No hashtags in comments.
5. Minimal emoji. Zero is usually right for first responder content. If you use one, make sure it fits the tone.
6. No "Stay safe out there" / "Be safe brother" (overused, sounds performative from a stranger).
7. No "Thank you for your service" alone. If you say it, pair with specifics.
8. No "As a [profession]..." framing of any kind.
9. No links or @mentions of Brian's accounts.
10. Sound like a 28-year Vegas neighbor, not a press release or service provider.
11. Match the post's tone. Somber for memorials. Warm for milestones. Casual for routine.
12. When in doubt, write less or skip the post.
