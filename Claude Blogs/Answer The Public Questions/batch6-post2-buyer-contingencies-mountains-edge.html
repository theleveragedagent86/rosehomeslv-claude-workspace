What Contingencies Should Mountain's Edge Sellers Understand?

Mountain's Edge sellers should understand four primary contingencies that appear in most purchase contracts: inspection, financing, appraisal, and home sale contingencies. Each gives buyers specific rights to withdraw from the transaction if conditions are not met, creating varying levels of risk for sellers evaluating competing offers.

The Four Common Contingencies Explained

Inspection contingencies give buyers 7 to 14 days to have the property professionally evaluated. About 75% of buyers include this contingency, allowing them to request repairs, renegotiate price, or withdraw if significant issues are discovered. The appraisal contingency protects buyers if the home appraises below the purchase price, since lenders will not finance more than the appraised value. In 2024, approximately 8% of appraisals came in lower than the contract price.

Financing contingencies provide buyers 30 to 60 days to secure mortgage approval. Even pre-approved buyers sometimes fail to obtain final loan approval, making this contingency a medium risk factor for sellers. Home sale contingencies, where buyers must sell their current home before purchasing yours, create the highest uncertainty and longest timelines.

Evaluating Contingency Risk

Offers with fewer contingencies generally present less risk of the deal falling through. In October 2025, 19% of buyers waived appraisal contingencies and 20% waived inspection contingencies to strengthen their offers in competitive situations. Cash buyers often present the cleanest offers since they eliminate financing and sometimes appraisal contingencies entirely.

Sellers can negotiate contingency terms rather than accepting them as written. Options include requesting shorter contingency periods, higher earnest money deposits, or partial contingency waivers. The strength of your negotiating position depends on local market conditions and buyer demand for Mountain's Edge homes.
