What Happens When Buyer Financing Falls Through in Mountain's Edge?

When a buyer's financing collapses after you have accepted their offer, your primary recourse depends on contract terms and timing. According to the National Association of Realtors, approximately 5 percent of contracts were terminated before reaching closing in late 2024. Redfin data showed that about 16 percent of homes under contract experienced cancellations in December 2024, the highest December rate on record. Understanding your protections and options helps you respond effectively when financing fails.

Why Financing Fails

Pre-approval letters do not guarantee final loan approval. Buyers can lose financing due to job changes, new debt accumulation (purchasing a car or opening credit cards), credit score drops, or income verification problems. Sometimes buyers change employers during escrow, triggering lender concerns. Appraisals coming in below the purchase price can also derail financing if the buyer cannot cover the difference. Even seemingly minor financial decisions during escrow can shift debt to income ratios enough to disqualify previously approved buyers.

Earnest Money and Contingencies

Financing contingencies protect buyers who cannot secure loans within specified timeframes, typically 30 to 45 days. If the buyer's loan is denied during the contingency period and proper notice is provided, they usually receive their earnest money back. However, once contingencies are removed, sellers have stronger rights. If financing fails after contingency removal, you may be entitled to keep the earnest money as compensation for lost time and opportunity. The escrow company holds these funds until both parties agree on disbursement, or until a dispute resolution process determines the outcome.

Protecting Yourself in Future Transactions

Sellers can reduce financing failure risk by scrutinizing buyer qualifications more thoroughly. Request buyers who are pre-underwritten rather than merely pre-approved, meaning a lender has already verified income, credit, debt, and employment. Consider shorter financing contingency periods to maintain deal momentum. Ask your agent to communicate directly with the buyer's lender to verify loan progress. Cash buyers eliminate financing risk entirely, though their offers may be lower.
