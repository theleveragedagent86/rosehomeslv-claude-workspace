Legal Documents Needed to Sell a House in Nevada

Nevada law requires specific disclosures and documentation when selling residential property. Understanding these requirements protects you legally and ensures a smooth transaction with your buyer.

Seller's Real Property Disclosure Form

The most critical document is Nevada's Seller's Real Property Disclosure Form (SRPD), required under NRS 113.130. This form must be completed by the seller personally and provided to buyers at least 10 days before closing. Your real estate agent cannot fill out this form on your behalf. The SRPD requires disclosure of any known conditions that materially affect the property's value or use in an adverse manner, including issues with systems, appliances, structural elements, and environmental concerns.

What Must Be Disclosed

Nevada's disclosure form covers extensive areas including sewer and septic systems, wells and pumps, sprinkler systems, solar heating, fireplaces, drainage issues, flood history, and structural concerns. You must disclose any past repairs, current defects you are aware of, and environmental hazards like mold, asbestos, or radon. However, Nevada law does not require you to disclose defects you genuinely do not know about.

Lead Based Paint Disclosure

Federal law requires sellers of homes built before 1978 to provide a Lead Based Paint Disclosure. This includes giving buyers the EPA's informational pamphlet, disclosing any known lead paint hazards, providing records of lead testing if available, and allowing buyers a 10 day period to conduct their own lead inspection.

Additional Required Disclosures

Depending on your property's location, you may need additional disclosures. Open Range Disclosure is required for properties adjacent to open range areas. Private Transfer Fee Obligation disclosure applies if your property is subject to such fees. Properties in Clark County near gaming enterprise districts require specific gaming related disclosures for new construction.

Purchase Agreement and Closing Documents

Beyond disclosures, you will need a properly executed purchase agreement outlining all terms of the sale. At closing, you will sign the deed transferring ownership, settlement statements detailing all financial transactions, and various affidavits and certifications required by the title company.
