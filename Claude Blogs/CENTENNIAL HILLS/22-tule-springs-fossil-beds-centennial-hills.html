Tule Springs Fossil Beds National Monument is a 22,650 acre preserve located directly north of Centennial Hills in Las Vegas, protecting Ice Age fossil deposits that date back as far as 200,000 years and offering residents a rare connection to prehistoric Nevada. ## What Is Tule Springs Fossil Beds National Monument? Established in 2014, Tule Springs Fossil Beds National Monument is one of the newest additions to the National Park Service system. The monument spans over 22,650 acres of desert terrain directly north of the Centennial Hills community, making it one of the most accessible national monuments for any residential neighborhood in the country. Scientists have uncovered fossils of Columbian mammoths, American lions, ancient camels, horses, bison, and dire wolves within its boundaries. The fossil record at Tule Springs covers a period stretching from roughly 200,000 to 3,000 years ago, painting a vivid picture of a very different Southern Nevada landscape. During the late Pleistocene era, this region was a lush wetland teeming with megafauna, a stark contrast to the arid desert residents see today. Ongoing paleontological research continues to yield new discoveries, and the monument serves as both a scientific research site and a public outdoor space. ## How Do You Get to Tule Springs Fossil Beds from Centennial Hills? Access to the monument is straightforward for Centennial Hills residents, with entry points along Moccasin Road and Durango Drive at the northern edge of the community. The drive from most Centennial Hills neighborhoods takes just a few minutes, making it easy to fit a visit into a weekend morning or an after school adventure. Trails within the monument range from short walks to longer hikes that explore the fossil bearing formations.
