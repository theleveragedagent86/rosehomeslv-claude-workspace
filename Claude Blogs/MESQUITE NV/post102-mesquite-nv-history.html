Mesquite NV was settled in 1880 by Mormon pioneers as "Mesquite Flat," part of Brigham Young's cotton and grape corridor along the Virgin River. The name was shortened to Mesquite in 1898, and the city officially incorporated in 1984. ## When Was Mesquite NV Founded? Mesquite NV history traces to 1880, when Mormon pioneers from Utah established a settlement they called Mesquite Flat along the Virgin River. The settlement succeeded only on the third attempt, after earlier efforts were washed out by Virgin River flooding, including a devastating June 1882 flood that destroyed the settlers' irrigation canals. Early construction relied on locally made adobe because the nearest lumber supply was 85 miles away and the nearest railroad sat 150 miles distant in Milford, Utah. The settlers came as part of Brigham Young's strategy to build a cotton and grape growing corridor through the warm Virgin River Valley. Despite brutal summer heat and recurring flood losses, the community persisted and slowly grew through the late 19th century. ## How Did Mesquite Become a City? By 1898 the name had been shortened from Mesquite Flat to simply Mesquite, and the town continued as a small agricultural community through most of the 20th century. The arrival of Interstate 15 in the 1970s transformed the town's economy by connecting it to Las Vegas and Salt Lake City traffic. Mesquite officially incorporated as a city in 1984, setting the stage for the casino, golf, and retirement development that followed in the 1990s and 2000s. The Si Redd era in the 1990s brought the Oasis resort and launched Mesquite's transition from farming outpost to tourism and retirement destination. Today the city is Nevada's fastest growing, with annual population growth near 3.7 percent. ## What Are Mesquite's Historic Sites? The Virgin Valley Heritage Museum preserves much of this pioneer history, including adobe artifacts, agricultural tools, and photographs from the early settlement period. Nearby Bunkerville, settled in 1877 just three years before Mesquite, remains a small rural community that shares the same Mormon pioneer roots.
