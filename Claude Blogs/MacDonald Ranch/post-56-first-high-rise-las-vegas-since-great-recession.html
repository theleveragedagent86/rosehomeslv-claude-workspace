The First High Rise in Las Vegas Since the Great Recession: Why It's in MacDonald Highlands

The Four Seasons Private Residences Las Vegas holds a distinction that speaks volumes about both the project and its location: it is the first residential high rise built in the Las Vegas Valley since the Great Recession of 2008. For more than 15 years, no developer was willing or able to bring a residential tower project to completion in a market that was devastated by the last condo boom and bust. That the project breaking that drought is a $1.3 billion Four Seasons development in MacDonald Highlands, not a Strip based casino tower, tells a revealing story about where Las Vegas luxury real estate is heading.

Why No High Rises for 15 Years

The Las Vegas high rise condo market was one of the Great Recession's most visible casualties. Projects like CityCenter (Veer Towers, Mandarin Oriental, Vdara), the Martin, and others were either completed just before or during the downturn, and many buyers who had purchased pre construction watched their investments lose 40 to 60 percent of value within 18 months. Some walked away from deposits entirely. The resulting trauma created a market where neither buyers nor lenders had appetite for new residential tower construction. Banks would not finance new condo projects. Buyers were skeptical of pre construction purchases. And developers turned to other property types (hotels, mixed use, industrial) where the economics were more favorable.

What Changed

Several factors converged to make the Four Seasons project possible where others remained on drawing boards. First, the buyer demographic shifted: the wave of California high net worth relocations to Nevada (up 45 percent between 2023 and 2026) created a buyer pool with both the financial capacity and the urgency (driven by California's 13.3 percent state income tax) to commit to pre construction purchases at premium prices. Second, the Four Seasons brand provided the credibility that a standalone developer could not: buyers trust Four Seasons to deliver and manage at a level that mitigates the risks associated with pre construction purchasing. Third, MacDonald Highlands as a location provided the residential infrastructure (guard gates, community, schools, DragonRidge Country Club) that Strip locations lack, attracting full time residents rather than speculative investors.

Why MacDonald Highlands, Not the Strip

The decision to build the first post recession high rise in MacDonald Highlands rather than on the Strip reflects a fundamental market insight: the buyer who is willing to spend $3.5 million to $30 million on a Las Vegas residence today is more likely to be a full time or primary resident seeking a lifestyle community than a speculator seeking a party pied a terre. MacDonald Highlands provides what the Strip cannot: quiet residential streets, guard gated security, mountain air at 2,500 to 2,700 feet elevation, world class golf at DragonRidge, top rated Henderson schools, and the established community of executives, entrepreneurs, and retirees who define the neighborhood. The Four Seasons brand brings the hospitality and service that the Strip is known for, but in a residential setting where you can actually live every day.

What This Means for the Las Vegas Market

The Four Seasons' success (over $725 million in sales, 70 percent sold before completion) signals that the Las Vegas luxury market has matured beyond its recession era trauma. The project has demonstrated that there is deep demand for branded luxury high rise living in Las Vegas, but the demand is driven by lifestyle buyers and tax motivated relocators rather than speculators. This is a healthier foundation for a luxury market than the pre 2008 speculation driven boom, and it suggests that additional branded and luxury high rise projects will follow in the coming years, both within MacDonald Highlands and elsewhere in the valley.
