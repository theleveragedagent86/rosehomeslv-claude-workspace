# Builder Contract Red Flags Every Las Vegas Buyer Should Know New construction contracts in Las Vegas are written entirely by the builder's legal team, which means every word in that document protects the builder first. Knowing what to look for before you sign can save you tens of thousands of dollars and months of frustration. ## The One-Sided Nature of Builder Contracts Las Vegas builder contracts are not standard purchase agreements. They are custom documents drafted by builders like Lennar, DR Horton, KB Home, and Toll Brothers to limit their liability at every turn. Unlike a resale transaction where both parties negotiate terms, the builder presents a take-it-or-leave-it contract that can run 50 to 100 pages. Many buyers sign without fully understanding what they have agreed to. The most important thing to understand is that these contracts are generally non-negotiable on their core terms, but that does not mean you cannot get concessions on price, incentives, or upgrade credits. Having a buyer's agent who regularly works with new construction gives you an advocate who understands how each builder operates and where there is room to negotiate. ## Red Flags to Read Carefully The first red flag is a right-to-delay clause. Most builder contracts allow the builder to extend the completion date by six months or more, often without financial penalty and sometimes without requiring notice. If your contract says the builder can delay indefinitely due to labor shortages, material costs, or market conditions, you could be locked into a purchase for a year or longer beyond the original close date. The second red flag is mandatory arbitration language. Many Las Vegas builder contracts require all disputes to go through private arbitration rather than court. This limits your rights under NRS Chapter 40, which governs construction defect claims in Nevada. Arbitration tends to favor builders and results in faster but lower settlements than litigation. The third red flag is the preferred lender clause. Some contracts offer attractive incentives only if you use the builder's in-house lender. That is legal, but the incentive can be structured to disappear if you shop rates. Always get a competing loan estimate before accepting any lender-tied incentive. The fourth red flag is a limited price protection window. If market values drop between signing and closing, you are typically locked into the original purchase price. Conversely, if values rise, the builder keeps the gain. Some contracts include clauses that allow the builder to adjust the price upward for materials costs, which is especially relevant given the tariff-related cost increases affecting new homes in 2026. ## What You Cannot Change vs. What You Can Structural specifications, community rules, and closing timelines are rarely negotiable in a meaningful way. However, upgrade credits, closing cost contributions, and rate buydowns are areas where buyers routinely see movement. DR Horton, Lennar, and Richmond American have all offered meaningful concessions in early 2026 to keep sales moving. ## Local Insight As a Las Vegas real estate specialist, Ryan Rose reviews builder contracts with buyers before they sign and flags clauses that could create problems down the road.
