# Should You Get a Home Inspection on a Brand New Home in Las Vegas? Yes, absolutely. Research shows that 9 out of 10 new construction homes have at least one significant issue discovered during a professional inspection, even when the builder's own inspector has signed off. Skipping the inspection on a brand new Las Vegas home is one of the most expensive mistakes a buyer can make. ## Why New Does Not Mean Perfect Builders in Las Vegas operate on tight schedules, and subcontractors work across multiple job sites simultaneously. That pace creates real opportunities for mistakes: improperly installed roofing, incomplete insulation in attic spaces, plumbing connections that are not fully sealed, and electrical work that passes a basic code check but still has problems. A third-party home inspector works only for you, not the builder, and has no incentive to overlook anything. Nevada's building inspectors from Clark County perform required code inspections at multiple stages, but those inspections are brief and focused on minimum code compliance. They are not a substitute for a full buyer's inspection that examines hundreds of individual systems and components. The two inspections serve completely different purposes. ## When to Schedule Your Inspection The best time to bring in your own inspector is during the pre-drywall stage, before the walls are closed. At this stage an inspector can visually examine framing, insulation, plumbing rough-ins, and electrical wiring that will be permanently hidden once drywall goes up. A pre-drywall inspection in Las Vegas typically costs between $200 and $400 and can reveal issues that would otherwise require tearing out finished walls to fix. The second critical inspection is the final walkthrough inspection, scheduled just before you close. This inspection covers all completed systems and finishes and costs between $310 and $730 in the Las Vegas market. Many buyers do both, and that combination gives you the most complete picture of your home's condition before you take title. ## What the Inspector Should Check A thorough new construction inspection in Las Vegas should include the HVAC system and ductwork, the roof and attic ventilation, all plumbing fixtures and water pressure, the electrical panel and outlets, windows and doors for proper sealing, and the foundation and concrete slab for visible cracking or uneven settling. In Las Vegas, attic ventilation is especially important because summer attic temperatures can reach 140 to 155 degrees Fahrenheit, and improper ventilation accelerates roof damage and strains the cooling system. Ask your inspector specifically about stucco cracks, which are common in the desert climate, and about any signs of moisture intrusion around windows and doors. Moisture problems may seem minor in a desert environment, but they can lead to significant damage if not caught early. ## What Happens If the Inspector Finds Something When your inspector identifies an issue, you have the right to bring it to the builder before closing. Most builders will correct legitimate defects discovered before the final blue tape walkthrough. If you are past closing, Nevada's warranty laws protect you, and the process for filing a warranty claim is straightforward as long as you document everything in writing. ## Local Insight As a Las Vegas real estate specialist, Ryan Rose strongly recommends that every new construction buyer hire an independent inspector, even when the builder offers their own inspection service.
