If you have kids in Clark County schools, your morning is about to look very different next year.

CCSD finalized a three-tier staggered bell schedule for the 2026-27 school year. Middle schools will start at 7:30 a.m. High schools will start at 8:30 a.m. Elementary schools will start at 9:15 a.m.

This is a big deal. CCSD had been the only school district in the entire country that started classes at 7:00 a.m. That meant some kids were catching buses before 6:00 in the morning. In a city where summer temperatures regularly hit 115 degrees and many parents work service industry hours, a 7:00 a.m. start created real problems for families.

The new schedule pushes every tier later. High schoolers get a full hour and a half more than the old start time. Elementary students do not start until after 9:00 a.m.

The research on later school start times is clear. Teenagers perform better academically. Attendance improves. Mental health outcomes get better. The American Academy of Pediatrics has been recommending later start times for years.

But research and real life are two different things.

Families with kids in multiple schools now have three different drop-off times to manage. A parent with a middle schooler and an elementary student is looking at a 7:30 drop-off and a 9:15 drop-off. That is a nearly two-hour window that makes getting to work on time much harder.

After-school schedules shift too. Later start means later dismissal. Sports practices push later. Homework starts later. Dinner gets pushed back. The entire family rhythm changes.

For working parents in the service industry, hospitality, healthcare, and construction, this is not a small adjustment. These are people who cannot just "flex their schedule" to accommodate a 9:15 a.m. start time.

The intent is good. The execution will determine whether this actually helps or just creates a different set of problems.

Will the new bell schedule help students, or will it just make life harder for working parents?

Behind the neon lights and construction cranes, Las Vegas small business owners are telling a very different story about how the city is doing.

At a Senate roundtable held in the Arts District, local business owners described rising costs, declining foot traffic, and growing financial strain. The numbers they shared paint a picture that the tourism marketing does not show.

Las Vegas visitation fell 7.5% in 2025 to about 38.5 million visitors. Canadian visitors specifically dropped 17.4%.

Those are not small declines for a city that runs on tourism dollars. Every hotel, restaurant, bar, and retail shop on and off the Strip depends on those visitors walking through their doors. When 7.5% of them stop coming, the impact cascades through the entire local economy.

Small businesses feel it first and hardest.

The big casinos have marketing budgets, loyalty programs, and convention business to absorb a slow year. A family-owned restaurant in the Arts District does not have those cushions. When foot traffic drops, they eat the loss directly.

Rising costs make it worse. Commercial rents in Las Vegas have been climbing. Insurance costs are up. Labor is more expensive. The math gets very tight when your revenue is declining while your expenses keep rising.

The senators at the roundtable listened. Whether anything comes from it is a different question. Federal action on local business relief tends to move slowly when it moves at all.

What the roundtable did accomplish was putting real faces and real numbers on a problem that is easy to ignore from the outside. Las Vegas looks like it is booming if you only see the new casino projects and restaurant openings. But underneath that surface, the businesses that give the city its texture are under real pressure.

A 7.5% drop in tourism is not a blip. If 2026 does not recover, some of these businesses will not survive to 2027.

How much can small businesses absorb before the character of entire neighborhoods starts changing?

## Story 18: The A's Stadium Just Hit a Major Construction Milestone on the South Strip

The A's Stadium Just Hit a Major Construction Milestone on the South Strip

The Las Vegas Athletics' $2 billion stadium is no longer just a hole in the ground. It just entered the steel phase, and it is real.

The 33,000-seat stadium on the south Strip has completed its foundation work and moved into steel erection. The project is on track for a 2028 opening. That means structural steel is now going up, which is the phase where a stadium starts looking like a stadium.

This project includes $380 million in taxpayer funding.

That number is worth repeating. Nevada taxpayers are putting $380 million toward a privately owned baseball team's new home. The remaining cost is funded privately, but the public contribution is substantial by any measure.

The A's relocated from Oakland after years of stadium disputes. Las Vegas beat out several other cities for the franchise, and the stadium deal was the centerpiece of that pitch.

For the south Strip, this project has the potential to completely transform the corridor. The area between Mandalay Bay and the welcome sign has been underdeveloped for years. A major sports venue with 33,000 seats would generate foot traffic, new restaurants, hotels, and entertainment that the south end has been missing.

That is the optimistic version. The less optimistic version asks whether a baseball stadium with 81 home games a year generates enough consistent activity to justify $2 billion in total investment and $380 million in taxpayer money.

Baseball stadiums in other cities have had mixed results as economic engines. Some, like San Diego's Petco Park, transformed their neighborhoods. Others became islands surrounded by parking lots.

Las Vegas has proven it can support major sports. The Raiders, the Golden Knights, and Formula 1 have all thrived. But baseball is a longer season with lower per-game attendance. Whether Vegas fans show up consistently for a Tuesday night game in July remains the unanswered question.

Should taxpayers foot the bill for a stadium they may never use?

## Story 19: Wagyu Factory Opens a New All-You-Can-Eat Spot in Boca Park

Wagyu Factory Opens a New All-You-Can-Eat Spot in Boca Park

An all-you-can-eat wagyu restaurant just opened in one of the most popular shopping centers in west Las Vegas. And yes, the A5 Japanese wagyu is included.

Wagyu Factory soft-opened at Boca Park, located at 740 S. Rampart Blvd. The restaurant offers tiered all-you-can-eat service that includes A5 Japanese wagyu, M9 Australian wagyu, and American wagyu options depending on the tier you choose.

All-you-can-eat wagyu sounds almost too good to be true. A5 Japanese wagyu alone typically runs $80 to $150 per pound at retail. The idea of sitting down and eating as much as you want challenges everything you know about how high-end beef is usually served.

The tiered pricing model is how they make it work. Lower tiers likely feature American wagyu and select cuts, while the top tier includes the A5 and M9 options at a premium price point. It is a smart structure that lets budget-conscious diners in the door while giving wagyu enthusiasts access to the top-end cuts.

Boca Park on Rampart Blvd. is a strong location choice. The shopping center serves Summerlin, the Lakes, and the west side of the valley. It already has a mix of popular dining options and consistent foot traffic from the surrounding neighborhoods.

Las Vegas has quietly become one of the best cities in the country for all-you-can-eat dining. Korean BBQ, hot pot, sushi, and now wagyu. The city's combination of competitive rents, a food-obsessed population, and a constant stream of tourists creates a market where these concepts can thrive.

The question for Wagyu Factory is whether the quality holds up at volume. All-you-can-eat restaurants live and die by consistency. The first visit might be incredible. The tenth visit determines whether it becomes a regular spot or a one-time experience.

For west Las Vegas residents, it is worth trying early while the restaurant is still in its soft opening phase and eager to impress.

Can an all-you-can-eat model deliver real wagyu quality, or does something have to give?

## Story 20: The Jonas Brothers' Restaurant at MGM Grand Is Closing, and That Makes Two This Month

The Jonas Brothers' Restaurant at MGM Grand Is Closing, and That Makes Two This Month

Nellie's Southern Kitchen, the Jonas Brothers family restaurant at MGM Grand, will close on or before June 1. That is the second restaurant closure at MGM Grand this month.

The restaurant opened on June 4, 2022, which means it lasted almost exactly three years. The closure comes as the lease at MGM Grand nears its end. Combined with the MGM Grand Buffet closing on May 31, the property is losing two dining concepts in the same month.

Nellie's was named after the Jonas family's grandmother and was positioned as a Southern comfort food concept. The celebrity connection was the primary draw. Southern food restaurants are not rare in Las Vegas, but one backed by one of the biggest pop acts in the world got attention.

Three years is a short run for a Strip restaurant, but it is not unusual. Celebrity-backed restaurants on the Strip have a mixed track record. Some, like Gordon Ramsay's concepts, become institutions. Others come and go without making a lasting impact.

The timing of this closure alongside the buffet is what makes it notable for MGM Grand.

Two dining closures in one month suggests that MGM Grand's parent company is actively restructuring the property's food and beverage lineup. When a casino loses two restaurants simultaneously, it is usually because ownership sees better uses for that square footage.

For MGM Grand visitors, the property is going to feel different this summer. The buffet was a decades-old staple. Nellie's was a newer addition. Losing both at once leaves gaps that have not been filled yet.

For the broader Strip dining scene, this fits the pattern. Restaurants are being treated as rotating concepts rather than permanent fixtures. Casinos are willing to cycle through brands, test new ideas, and move on quickly if the numbers do not work.

The question is whether constant turnover improves the dining experience or just makes the Strip feel less reliable.

How many restaurant closures can one casino have before visitors stop trusting the dining options?
