## Story 9: Las Vegas Cut Its Per Capita Water Use by 58 Percent While Growing by 876,000 People

Every summer the Southern Nevada Water Authority enforces watering restrictions, and most people treat it as an inconvenience. The actual numbers behind those rules tell a much more interesting story.

Mandatory summer restrictions are in effect from May 1 through August 31. The rules are straightforward: no outdoor watering on Sundays, and no watering between 11 a.m. and 7 p.m. on any day.

Since 2002, per capita water use in Southern Nevada has dropped 58%. The population during that same period grew by 876,000 people.

That combination, sharply lower per-person consumption alongside a population nearly a million larger, is one of the most significant conservation achievements of any major American city in the past two decades.

It happened because of a sustained, multi-decade effort: tiered pricing, grass removal rebates, strict enforcement, and public education. The restrictions are not the whole story, but they are part of a culture shift in how this valley thinks about water.

Las Vegas sits in the Mojave Desert and draws most of its water from Lake Mead, which has been under stress from drought and overuse across the entire Colorado River basin for years.

The improvements since 2002 bought this region time. Whether that time is used wisely depends on what happens next, both locally and at the federal level with the Colorado River Compact.

The three fields losing the shortage label are Elementary K-5, Secondary English 7-12, and Elementary Counseling. The district says it now has a surplus of qualified candidates in all three areas.

The 160 affected teachers now have three choices: stay in their current roles without the double-income benefit, transfer to a field that still carries a shortage designation, or resign.

Double-dipping in public employment is a sensitive topic. These teachers were not breaking any rules. The district created the arrangement specifically to retain experienced educators in hard-to-fill positions. That was a legitimate policy goal.

But when the shortage no longer exists, continuing to pay the premium is harder to justify, especially inside a district that just approved more than 1,200 job eliminations in its budget.

This is not a water expansion project. It is a redundancy project. Southern Nevada's current water delivery system has single points of failure that would be catastrophic in a major infrastructure incident. This pipeline eliminates that vulnerability.

$2 billion is a significant federal investment in Southern Nevada infrastructure. It reflects recognition at the federal level that Las Vegas is now a major American city whose water security is a national concern, not just a local one.
