RTC Proposes 50% Bus Fare Hike and Warns of Major Route Cuts Across Clark County

The Regional Transportation Commission of Southern Nevada wants to raise your bus fare, and if the increase does not happen, tens of thousands of daily riders across Clark County could lose their routes entirely. The RTC announced a proposal to increase the single-ride bus fare from $2 to $3, a 50% jump, while also raising monthly passes from $65 to $80. Officials say the agency faces a $118.5 million annual funding shortfall, and without new revenue, up to 40% of bus routes could be eliminated by 2028.

If you ride the bus to work, the doctor, the grocery store, or school, this proposal affects you directly. And even if you drive, what happens to public transit in this valley shapes traffic, housing, and neighborhood access for everyone. The public comment window is open right now, and it closes June 23, 2026.

What Happened

In May 2026, the RTC formally proposed two fare changes. The first would raise the single-ride fare from $2 to $3. That is an increase of one dollar per trip. For someone who rides twice a day, five days a week, that adds up to $40 more per month out of pocket. The second change would raise the monthly unlimited pass from $65 to $80, a $15 increase per month.

These would be the first fare increases in over 15 years. The RTC has held the $2 single-ride fare for a very long time while costs have climbed steadily around it. Fuel, maintenance, driver wages, and insurance have all gone up. Farebox revenue has not kept pace. That gap is now $118.5 million per year.

The RTC is the agency that runs bus service throughout Clark County. Its fleet includes well-known routes like the Deuce, the double-decker bus that runs the length of the Las Vegas Strip, and the SDX, which connects the Strip to downtown Las Vegas. Beyond those tourist-facing lines, the RTC operates dozens of local routes that carry workers, students, seniors, and low-income residents all across the valley. North Las Vegas, Henderson, Spring Valley, downtown, East Las Vegas, and the suburbs are all connected through this network.

The $118.5 million shortfall is driven by two forces working against each other at the same time. Operating costs keep rising. And farebox revenue, meaning the money collected from riders, has stayed flat or fallen short of what it needs to be. The RTC receives funding from a combination of federal grants, local government contributions, and what riders pay at the fare box. When any one of those legs weakens, the whole system strains.

The warning attached to this proposal is serious. If the fare increase is not approved and no other solution is found, officials say up to 40% of bus routes could be cut by 2028. That is not a minor adjustment. Losing 40% of routes would fundamentally reshape who can get around in Southern Nevada without a car.

The public comment period is open through June 23, 2026, at rtcsnv.com/transitupdates. This is your chance to tell the RTC what you think before a final decision is made.

Why It Matters to Las Vegas Residents

It is easy to overlook public transit if you drive everywhere. But in Clark County, a large share of residents depend on the bus as their only reliable way to get around. Low-income workers, seniors on fixed incomes, people with disabilities, teenagers, and anyone who cannot afford or cannot drive a car all depend on the RTC network.

A 50% increase in the single-ride fare hits hardest for people who buy tickets trip by trip rather than a monthly pass. These are often the riders who cannot afford to pay a month upfront. If you take two buses to get to work and two buses to get home, that is four trips per day, or roughly 80 trips per month. At $2 per ride, that is $160 a month. At $3 per ride, it becomes $240 a month. That is $80 more every single month just to get to your job. For someone earning a minimum wage or slightly above it in Las Vegas, that is a significant bite out of the budget.

The monthly pass increase from $65 to $80 is smaller in percentage terms. But $15 extra per month is real money for households that are already stretching their paychecks. Many transit-dependent families in Clark County spend a meaningful portion of their income on basic transportation, and every dollar matters.

Now consider what 40% route cuts would actually look like on the ground. Not every neighborhood would feel it equally. Some areas of the valley have multiple overlapping routes and would still have options. But other neighborhoods, particularly lower-income areas in North Las Vegas and East Las Vegas, parts of Henderson away from major corridors, and outer suburban communities in Spring Valley or the southwest valley, are served by just one or two routes. If those routes get cut, residents in those areas lose their connection to everything. Work. Medical appointments. Grocery stores. Schools.

Seniors who no longer drive and rely on fixed-route buses would face enormous disruption. Healthcare access is a particular concern. Las Vegas has spread out geographically, and many medical facilities are not walkable from residential areas. A senior who misses a bus connection to a dialysis appointment or a pharmacy faces a health consequence, not just an inconvenience.

Workers in hospitality and service industries, which make up a huge part of the Las Vegas economy, often work overnight or early morning shifts. Bus service frequency at off-peak hours is already limited. Cuts could eliminate some of those late-night runs entirely, forcing workers to find rides they cannot afford or face discipline at work for being late.

The people least able to absorb a fare increase are also the people most likely to lose routes if no increase is approved. There is no good outcome here without some kind of action. That is what makes this proposal so difficult for so many riders.

Background and History

The RTC has not raised single-ride bus fares in more than 15 years. That is a remarkably long stretch of time to hold a price steady, especially in a region that has experienced significant growth and rising costs. When the $2 fare was set, gas was cheaper, operating costs were lower, and the valley's population was different. None of those things are still true.

The RTC is funded through three main sources. Federal transportation grants provide a major portion of the operating budget. Local government contributions from Clark County, the City of Las Vegas, Henderson, North Las Vegas, and other municipalities add to the pot. Farebox revenue, the money riders pay to board the bus, makes up the remainder. The exact balance of these three sources shifts year to year depending on federal priorities, local budgets, and ridership levels.

Federal transportation funding has become less predictable in recent years. Policy changes at the national level have created uncertainty for transit agencies across the country, and the RTC is not alone in facing this challenge. When federal grants shrink or are delayed, agencies have to find other ways to fill the gap. Local governments in Southern Nevada are also under fiscal pressure, managing their own budget challenges with competing priorities.

Meanwhile, ridership never fully recovered to pre-2020 levels across many transit systems in the United States, and Las Vegas is no exception. Fewer riders means less farebox revenue, even when fares stay the same. Add rising labor costs and higher fuel and maintenance expenses, and the math becomes very difficult very fast.

The $118.5 million annual shortfall did not appear overnight. It built up over years as costs rose and revenue stayed flat. The 15-year freeze on fares was partly a political choice and partly a practical one, designed to protect low-income riders from increases during and after the Great Recession and the COVID-19 economic disruptions. But that long freeze has a cost of its own, and that cost is now coming due.

What Happens Next

The public comment period is the most immediate and important step right now. The RTC is collecting feedback from riders, residents, businesses, and community organizations through June 23, 2026. You can submit a comment online at rtcsnv.com/transitupdates. Comments can also be submitted in writing or at public meetings, which the RTC typically holds throughout the valley to reach different communities.

After the comment period closes, the RTC board will review what they heard from the public and weigh it alongside the financial data. The board is made up of elected officials from the local jurisdictions the RTC serves, including Clark County commissioners and representatives from Las Vegas, Henderson, and North Las Vegas. They have the authority to approve or reject the fare increase proposal.

No final vote date has been publicly announced as of this writing. Decisions of this magnitude typically go through multiple board sessions, and the RTC may also release additional analysis or alternatives before a final vote. Riders and advocates who care about the outcome should watch for meeting announcements on the RTC website.

If the fare increase is approved, it would likely take effect sometime after the vote, giving the agency time to update systems, signs, and communications. If it is rejected, or if no alternative funding solution is found, the RTC would have to begin the difficult process of identifying which routes to cut and developing a service reduction plan. The 40% figure is the projected level of cuts needed by 2028 if the funding gap is not addressed. That process would also require public input under federal transit planning rules.

There is also the possibility that the RTC pursues additional funding from other sources, negotiates adjusted contributions from local governments, or implements a combination of smaller fare increases and targeted service reductions rather than the full proposed increase. Transit agencies often go through several rounds of public input and internal analysis before settling on a final plan. The current proposal is the opening position, not necessarily the final one.

What is certain is that the status quo is not financially sustainable. Something will change. The question is what, and who bears the cost.

Ryan's Take

From a real estate perspective, public transit quality is one of the most underrated factors in how neighborhoods develop and hold their value over time. A strong bus network makes a neighborhood more accessible. More accessible neighborhoods tend to attract more residents, support more small businesses, and draw more investment. When transit gets cut, the opposite can happen. Neighborhoods that were already struggling can fall further behind.

In Las Vegas, this matters in a very specific way. The valley has grown fast and spread wide. Car ownership is nearly universal for middle-class and above households, but a large slice of renters, particularly in areas like North Las Vegas, East Las Vegas, and parts of Henderson, either do not own a car or rely on transit for a significant portion of their daily trips. Renters make up a huge portion of Clark County households, and renters are disproportionately transit-dependent.

When transit service gets cut in a neighborhood, renters face pressure to move somewhere with better access or pay more for rides. That can accelerate turnover in rental housing and make it harder for landlords to fill units in affected areas. It can also suppress the attractiveness of multifamily development near corridors that lose service.

For buyers and sellers, I always factor in transit access when evaluating a property's long-term value and rental potential. A 50% fare increase is not a small thing for people on tight budgets. And losing 40% of routes is not a small thing for any neighborhood that depends on those routes. This is a story worth following closely no matter where you live in Clark County.

What You Can Do

The most direct action you can take right now is to submit a public comment. The deadline is June 23, 2026. Go to rtcsnv.com/transitupdates to find the comment form, upcoming public meeting dates, and information about which routes are being considered for changes.

If you are a regular bus rider, your comment carries real weight. Tell the RTC which routes you use, why you depend on them, and how a fare increase or route cut would affect your daily life. Specific, personal stories are more influential than general opinions. If you know neighbors, coworkers, or family members who ride the bus, encourage them to comment too. Transit agencies pay attention to the volume and specificity of public feedback.

If you want to know which routes might be at risk, the RTC website has maps and route information. Routes that run in lower-density areas, run less frequently, or have lower ridership are generally the most vulnerable to cuts. If you live in or near a community that is served by only one or two routes, it is worth paying particular attention.

You can also contact your local elected officials. Clark County commissioners, Las Vegas City Council members, and Henderson and North Las Vegas council members all have seats on or influence over the RTC board. Let them know where you stand. They represent you, and this is exactly the kind of issue where constituent contact makes a difference.

Finally, if you work for a business or organization that employs people who ride the bus, consider whether your company wants to weigh in. Employers in hospitality, healthcare, retail, and other sectors that rely on transit-dependent workers have a direct stake in the outcome. Business voices carry weight in these discussions.

The comment window closes June 23, 2026. Act before it closes.
