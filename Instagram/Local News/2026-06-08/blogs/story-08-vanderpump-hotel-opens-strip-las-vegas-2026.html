Vanderpump Hotel Opens on the Las Vegas Strip, Replacing The Cromwell

What Is the Vanderpump Hotel

Las Vegas has seen its share of grand openings over the decades, but the debut of the Vanderpump Hotel on May 29, 2026, stands out as something genuinely different from the mega-resort openings that typically define the Strip. This is a 188-room boutique property, and that smaller footprint is not a limitation. It is the entire point.

The hotel was designed by television personality, restaurateur and entrepreneur Lisa Vanderpump in partnership with designer Nick Alain. Together, they applied what Vanderpump describes as an "industrial romantic" aesthetic throughout the property. The phrase might sound like a contradiction, but the result is a visual identity that blends exposed structural elements with lush, opulent details. Think warm metals, rich fabrics, moody lighting and design choices that feel personal rather than corporate.

The property sits in the space formerly occupied by The Cromwell, a standalone boutique casino hotel that had carved out its own identity on the Strip since 2014. When Vanderpump and Alain took over the building, they did not simply swap logos and refresh the paint. The transformation involved a redesigned casino floor and a top-to-bottom rethinking of the guest experience.

One of the most talked-about features at the Vanderpump Hotel is Soleia, a rooftop pool club that positions itself as a destination in its own right. Rooftop amenities have become increasingly competitive on the Strip, and Soleia enters that conversation with the full weight of the Vanderpump brand behind it. For visitors looking to spend time above the city's noise with views of the Strip and the surrounding valley, Soleia is expected to be among the new property's biggest draws.

Opening weekend rates for the Vanderpump Hotel started at around $419 per night, placing it firmly in the premium boutique category. That price point reflects the deliberate positioning of the property: not the sprawling convention-centric mega-resort, but an intimate, design-forward stay that appeals to guests who want something curated and distinctive from their Las Vegas experience.

Why This Opening Matters to Las Vegas

Every hotel opening on the Strip matters to Las Vegas to some degree, but the Vanderpump Hotel opening carries a particular kind of significance because of what it signals about where the city's hospitality industry is heading.

Las Vegas has long been defined by volume. Bigger casinos, more rooms, larger entertainment venues, higher capacities. The model worked, and it built an economy that the entire region of Clark County depends upon. But the guest profile that drives the Strip is changing. A growing segment of travelers, particularly younger visitors and those with higher discretionary budgets, are looking for experiences that feel exclusive and intentional rather than overwhelming. The boutique hotel model speaks directly to that audience.

Lisa Vanderpump's name carries genuine recognition well beyond Las Vegas. Her restaurants and bars have drawn loyal followings across the country, and her television presence has introduced her brand to millions of people who have never set foot in one of her venues. That built-in audience is part of what makes the Vanderpump Hotel notable. It arrives with a pre-existing fan base and a level of social media visibility that most hotel openings do not have on day one.

For the Las Vegas hospitality industry, the opening also demonstrates that the Strip corridor remains a viable location for reinvention. The Cromwell site did not sit empty or struggle through a prolonged transition. Instead, it attracted a high-profile concept with real investment behind it, and the result was a property that opened with considerable buzz. That pattern is healthy for the broader market and for the neighborhoods and communities throughout Clark County that depend on tourism revenues flowing through the Strip.

The hospitality sector is one of the primary economic engines of the Las Vegas Valley. When major properties open, expand or reinvent themselves, those changes ripple outward. They influence hotel room rates across the market, they create jobs, and they affect how the region is perceived by travelers weighing Las Vegas against other destinations. The Vanderpump Hotel is one data point in that larger picture, and it is a positive one.

The Cromwell and What Has Changed

To understand what the Vanderpump Hotel represents, it helps to know a little about what was there before. The Cromwell opened in 2014 as the first standalone boutique casino hotel on the Las Vegas Strip. Before that renovation, the building had operated under several identities going back decades, including as the Barbary Coast and later Bill's Gamblin' Hall and Saloon. The Cromwell was an attempt to bring a more curated, European-influenced aesthetic to the Strip.

The Cromwell had its supporters, and the property maintained a following during its run. Its rooftop venue, Drai's, became one of the more well-known nightlife destinations on the Strip. But the brand never quite achieved the cultural footprint that might have insulated it from eventual transition. The property changed hands and underwent the kind of quiet repositioning that often precedes a more dramatic reinvention.

What Vanderpump and Alain brought to the space is a sharper identity and a more cohesive vision. The "industrial romantic" design language that defines the hotel extends from the public areas through the guest rooms and up to Soleia. Where The Cromwell applied a relatively neutral boutique-hotel sensibility, the Vanderpump Hotel applies a specific point of view. Guests who book a stay know exactly what kind of experience they are walking into, and that clarity is part of what boutique hospitality does well.

The redesigned casino floor is worth noting separately. Casinos on the Strip are not just amenities. They are operational cores that drive a significant share of hotel revenue. Rethinking a casino floor requires balancing aesthetic goals with the practical realities of gaming traffic and layout. The Vanderpump team made those changes as part of the overall renovation, which suggests a comprehensive approach to the transformation rather than a cosmetic one.

For longtime visitors to Las Vegas, the loss of a familiar property is always worth acknowledging. The Cromwell served a real audience during its years on the Strip, and change on this corridor is not always welcomed without some nostalgia for what came before. But the nature of the Strip is reinvention, and the Vanderpump Hotel represents that tradition continuing.

What You Can Expect If You Visit

If you are planning a visit to the Vanderpump Hotel, or simply curious about what the experience offers, the key differentiator starts the moment you arrive. The 188-room count keeps the property intimate compared to the sprawling towers that dominate much of the Strip. Checking in does not mean navigating through thousands of other guests or waiting in a lobby that feels like a small city. The scale is intentionally human.

The guest rooms reflect the "industrial romantic" design philosophy that guides the whole property. Nick Alain, whose work spans both commercial and residential design, brought a detailed approach to the interiors. Guests should expect spaces that feel considered rather than generic, with materials and finishes that support the overall aesthetic. The design is meant to be felt as much as seen.

Soleia, the rooftop pool club, is positioned as one of the signature experiences at the property. Rooftop venues on the Strip compete intensely for daytime and evening visitors, and the best ones manage to create a sense of separation from the city below while keeping the skyline visible. Soleia's location and the Vanderpump brand both contribute to its appeal, and it is likely to draw visitors who are not hotel guests as well as those who are staying on property.

The casino floor is part of the guest experience as well, and the redesign is meant to integrate with the broader aesthetic of the hotel rather than feeling like a separate operation dropped into the building. Boutique casinos operate differently from mega-resort floors in terms of atmosphere and pace, and that suits the audience the Vanderpump Hotel is targeting.

Opening weekend rates starting at around $419 per night set expectations clearly. This is not a budget stay or a mid-market property angling for convention business. The Vanderpump Hotel is reaching for a guest who values design, brand identity and a curated experience, and is willing to pay a premium for it on the Strip. Visitors who fall into that category will likely find that the property delivers on its premise.

For those planning a trip to Las Vegas in 2026, the Vanderpump Hotel is worth putting on the shortlist, particularly for couples, design enthusiasts, or anyone who has followed Vanderpump's work in restaurants and hospitality. The property offers something genuinely different from the major resort options on the Strip, and that distinction is increasingly valuable in a market where travelers have no shortage of choices.
