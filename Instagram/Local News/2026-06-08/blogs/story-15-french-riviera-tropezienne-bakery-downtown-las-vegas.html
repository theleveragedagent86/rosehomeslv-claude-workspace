French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery to Open Third Location in Iconic Downtown Space

Downtown Las Vegas is getting a new reason to linger over coffee and pastry this summer. French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery is preparing to open its third Las Vegas location inside the historic building at 401 S. Sixth St., a space that held one of the most significant French fine-dining restaurants in Nevada history for nearly three decades. The address has sat quiet for the better part of two decades, but an extensive renovation completed in 2025 has brought it back to life, and the arrival of French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery marks the beginning of a new chapter for one of downtown's most recognizable corners.

For anyone paying attention to downtown Las Vegas right now, this opening is more than just a new place to grab a croissant. It is a signal. It tells you something about where this neighborhood is heading, how seriously operators are taking the residential and visitor foot traffic that has built up over the past several years, and why the ground-floor spaces in this part of Clark County are starting to attract names that would not have looked twice at downtown a decade ago.

If you are thinking about buying, renting, or investing anywhere in the downtown corridor, the kind of business activity coming to 401 S. Sixth St. is exactly the type of indicator worth watching.

What Is French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery

French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery is a Las Vegas-based bakery concept built around the flavors and traditions of the French Riviera. The name itself nods to the Tropezienne, a brioche-based cake filled with cream that originated in Saint-Tropez and became one of the most beloved pastries in southern France. That single item tells you something about the identity of the brand: it is rooted in a specific, regional French baking tradition rather than a generic European cafe aesthetic.

The bakery already operates two locations in the Las Vegas Valley, and the downtown opening at 401 S. Sixth St. will be its third. Expanding to a third location in any market takes confidence, and choosing a site as storied and visible as the former Andre's building suggests the ownership group sees real, sustained demand in the downtown core. This is not a pop-up or a soft launch. It is a full commitment to one of downtown Las Vegas's most historically weighted addresses.

French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery fits naturally into the kind of neighborhood that downtown Las Vegas is becoming. The blocks surrounding the Arts District, the Fremont East corridor, and the stretch of residential development along the northern edge of downtown have all attracted residents who want walkable access to quality food and coffee. A French bakery of this caliber, occupying a thoughtfully renovated building, is precisely the type of anchor that helps a neighborhood feel complete.

The Historic Space at 401 S. Sixth Street

The building at 401 S. Sixth St. carries more history per square foot than almost any other restaurant address in Las Vegas. Tucked into a residential-scale block south of Fremont Street, the structure does not look like a typical Vegas dining destination. There is no neon marquee, no valet stand spilling onto a boulevard. What it has instead is character: a house-like facade, mature landscaping, and the quiet sense that something important used to happen there.

That something was Andre's, and we will get to the full story of that restaurant in a moment. What matters here is the physical space itself and what the renovation has done to prepare it for a new generation of diners and pastry lovers.

An extensive renovation was completed in 2025. The nature of a careful renovation in a building like this one typically involves preserving the bones and the details that give the space its identity while updating the systems, the surfaces, and the functionality to meet current standards. After sitting largely dormant since Andre's closed on New Year's Eve 2008, the building needed real work. The fact that a renovation was completed and a quality operator like French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery is moving in suggests the space has been brought back to a condition worthy of its history.

For the neighborhood, a renovated building at 401 S. Sixth St. with an active, well-regarded tenant is a meaningful improvement. Vacant or underutilized buildings pull down the energy of a block. Active, well-maintained ones lift it. The return of this address to a productive use is good news not just for food lovers but for anyone who owns property nearby or is considering buying in the area.

The Story of Andre's and Why It Mattered

To understand why this opening carries weight in downtown Las Vegas, you need to know something about what Andre's was and what it meant to the city.

Andre's was a pioneering French fine-dining restaurant that operated at 401 S. Sixth St. for nearly thirty years. It closed on New Year's Eve 2008, making the building's silence a fixture of the downtown landscape for roughly seventeen years before the 2025 renovation. In a city that has always been quick to bulldoze and rebuild, a building that held a single beloved restaurant for three decades is genuinely rare.

Andre's was considered one of the foremost fine-dining experiences in Las Vegas during its run. In a city where dining out is treated as a form of entertainment and spectacle, Andre's offered something different: a serious, classically French experience in an intimate setting that felt more like a European townhouse than a casino showroom. For Las Vegas in the 1980s and 1990s, that was a radical proposition.

The restaurant helped establish a benchmark for what fine dining in Nevada could look like. It attracted a clientele that ranged from local regulars to visitors who specifically sought it out as a counterpoint to the Strip's more theatrical offerings. When it closed, it left behind a physical space so layered with memory and reputation that no ordinary successor was ever going to feel right.

French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery is not trying to replicate what Andre's was. It is a bakery, not a white-tablecloth restaurant, and that distinction matters. What it is doing is bringing a French culinary sensibility back to a space that was built around one. There is a coherence to that choice that feels intentional, and it honors the address without attempting an impossible imitation.

What Downtown Las Vegas Looks Like Now

The downtown Las Vegas that French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery is entering in summer 2026 is a very different place from the one that watched Andre's close in 2008.

The Arts District, which runs along the blocks surrounding Casino Center Boulevard and extends south toward the city's older residential neighborhoods, has grown into one of the most active cultural and culinary clusters in Clark County. First Friday draws thousands of people to its galleries, vendors, and performance spaces on a monthly basis. The density of independent restaurants, cocktail bars, coffee shops, and creative studios has reached a point where the Arts District is genuinely competing with other urban neighborhoods nationally for attention and talent.

Fremont East has continued to develop its identity as a nightlife and dining corridor, with a mix of established bars and newer restaurants filling in gaps that existed even a few years ago. New cocktail concepts and chef-driven restaurants have opened, been celebrated, and drawn national press coverage. The pipeline shows no signs of thinning.

Residential development in the area has added density that supports exactly this kind of food and beverage activity. Apartment buildings and mixed-use projects have brought residents who want to walk to quality neighborhood spots rather than drive to the Strip. That demographic shift is the engine behind much of what is opening downtown right now, including French Riviera Tropezienne Bakery.

For people who already own homes or investment properties in the downtown corridor, this trajectory is encouraging. For people who are still evaluating whether to buy in the area, the activity at places like 401 S. Sixth St. is the kind of real-world evidence that matters more than any market report. Neighborhoods where this caliber of operator is making multi-year commitments are neighborhoods worth taking seriously.

You might also be interested in some of the other restaurant and bar openings reshaping the downtown corridor. Prowl Cocktail Bar recently opened in the Las Vegas Arts District, adding another quality option within walking distance of 401 S. Sixth St. Lilli by Chef Tyler Vorce has moved to Chinatown, reflecting the continued movement of serious culinary talent into neighborhoods outside the Strip. And Maroon by Kwame Onwuachi has opened at Sahara Las Vegas, bringing a James Beard Award-winning chef's vision to a property that anchors the northern end of the corridor. These openings, taken together, tell the story of a city whose dining culture is maturing rapidly.
