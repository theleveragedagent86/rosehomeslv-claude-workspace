Hayworth Restaurant Opens in Henderson, Brings Top Chef Alum to the Suburbs

Hayworth at 1450 W. Horizon Ridge Pkwy. in Henderson, Nevada, opened February 26, 2026.

What Hayworth Is and Why Henderson Is Excited

Henderson has had no shortage of good places to eat over the years, but a certain kind of restaurant has historically required a drive to the Strip or downtown Las Vegas to find. The kind of place where a credentialed chef puts a personal story on the plate, where the ingredients carry real intention, and where a weeknight dinner can feel like an occasion. That changed on February 26, 2026, when Hayworth opened its doors at 1450 W. Horizon Ridge Pkwy.

The 80-seat restaurant is the debut project of chef Alex Reznik, a familiar face to anyone who follows competitive cooking television. In a market where celebrity chef concepts tend to cluster around casino corridors, Hayworth is something genuinely different. It is a suburban fine dining room built not around a brand or a franchise but around a single chef's heritage, memories, and craft. For Henderson residents who have watched the city grow into one of the most livable communities in Clark County, this feels like a milestone worth paying attention to.

Las Vegas Weekly published a full review in June 2026, and the early word since opening has confirmed what the concept promised. Hayworth is not a casual neighborhood spot or a trendy pop-up. It is a proper restaurant, the kind that gives Henderson residents another strong reason to stay close to home when they want to celebrate, entertain clients, or simply eat very well on a Saturday night.

Who Is Chef Alex Reznik

Alex Reznik is not a first-time restaurateur hoping to learn on the job. He arrived in Henderson with a resume that is hard to argue with. Reznik competed on Season 7 of Top Chef, the long-running Bravo competition series that has launched or confirmed the careers of some of the most respected chefs working in American restaurants today. Appearing on that show at all signals a level of technical ability and culinary vision that sets a candidate apart from the broad field of restaurant talent.

Beyond his Top Chef appearance, Reznik earned a notable distinction on Beat Bobby Flay, the Food Network series in which chefs challenge the legendary New York chef Bobby Flay in a head-to-head cook-off. Reznik won. That is not a trivial accomplishment. Flay has defended his title against a very long list of talented competitors, and the chefs who beat him tend to carry that credit for the rest of their careers.

What makes Reznik's arrival in Henderson particularly compelling is the story behind the food. He has built Hayworth around his Jewish and Eastern European heritage, drawing from culinary traditions that are rich, deeply specific, and relatively underrepresented in the Las Vegas restaurant scene. The dishes on the menu are not nostalgic recreations of deli food or crowd-pleasing approximations of brisket. They are fine dining interpretations of a cuisine shaped by centuries of culture, migration, and family memory. That kind of specificity is exactly what separates a great restaurant from a good one.

What Is on the Menu at Hayworth

Three dishes in particular have defined early conversations about Hayworth, and each one illustrates what Reznik is doing at a conceptual level.

The beef tongue agnolotti is the kind of dish that rewards open-minded diners. Beef tongue has deep roots in Eastern European Jewish cooking, where it was a valued cut prepared with care because nothing went to waste. In Reznik's hands, it becomes the filling for agnolotti, a Northern Italian pasta shape that requires real technical skill to produce. The combination is unexpected but makes cultural sense. It speaks to the way immigrant food traditions blend and adapt over generations. For diners who have not tried tongue before, the pasta form lowers the barrier to entry while still delivering the full flavor of the preparation.

The king salmon-topped potato latkes reframe another iconic dish. Latkes are among the most recognizable foods in the Ashkenazi Jewish tradition, and virtually everyone in the restaurant's dining room will have some point of reference for them. By pairing them with king salmon, Reznik elevates a humble, familiar form into something genuinely elegant without losing the emotional connection that latkes carry. The dish bridges comfort and sophistication in a way that is genuinely difficult to achieve.

The 24-hour slow-cooked wagyu brisket is perhaps the most direct expression of Reznik's background. Brisket is the defining celebration meat in Jewish cooking, the centerpiece of Friday night dinners and holiday tables, the dish that grandmothers are remembered by. A 24-hour cook on wagyu beef takes that foundation and pushes it as far as technique and ingredient quality will allow. The result is almost certainly unlike any brisket most diners have had before, not because it abandons tradition but because it honors it with exceptional care.

Taken together, these three dishes tell you everything you need to know about what Hayworth is trying to be. This is heritage cooking filtered through serious culinary training and real creative ambition. The restaurant is 80 seats, which keeps the experience intimate and allows the kitchen to maintain the attention to detail that this kind of food requires.

What Horizon Ridge Parkway Looks Like as a Dining Destination

The address at 1450 W. Horizon Ridge Pkwy. is worth thinking about for a moment. Horizon Ridge Parkway runs through one of Henderson's more established and well-trafficked commercial corridors. It connects established neighborhoods to the Green Valley area and sits within easy reach of some of the city's most desirable residential communities. The population density along this corridor is precisely the kind that can sustain a serious independent restaurant. Residents here tend to be well-traveled, income-stable, and accustomed to eating at quality restaurants, whether that means heading to the Strip on a special occasion or finding good food closer to home.

The opening of Hayworth at this location reflects a broader shift that has been building in Henderson and across Clark County for several years. The assumption that fine dining belongs exclusively to the resort corridor is becoming less and less accurate. As Henderson has grown into a city of its own rather than simply a suburb of Las Vegas, the demand for serious restaurant experiences at the local level has grown with it. Hayworth is a direct response to that demand.

For residents of the Green Valley Ranch area, MacDonald Ranch, or any of the neighborhoods that line the corridor between Eastern Avenue and the McCullough Range, Hayworth means a top-tier dining option within a comfortable drive. For people considering a move to Henderson from other parts of the valley or from out of state, it is one more piece of evidence that the city offers a quality of life that extends well beyond its residential streets.

Henderson is the second-largest city in Nevada. It has its own government, its own infrastructure, its own identity. The growth of its restaurant scene is part of a larger story about what kind of city Henderson is becoming, and Hayworth fits squarely into the most compelling version of that story.

Why Henderson's Restaurant Scene Keeps Getting Better

Hayworth did not arrive in a vacuum. The Henderson dining scene has been maturing steadily, and the opening of a restaurant of this caliber reflects that maturity rather than simply creating it. There is now a critical mass of residents who want serious food, a commercial infrastructure along key corridors that can support independent operators, and enough broader attention on the Las Vegas valley dining scene to attract talented chefs who might previously have looked only at the Strip.

Clark County as a whole benefits from this dynamic. Las Vegas remains one of the premier dining destinations in the country, but the concentration of that excellence in resort corridors has always meant that most of the valley's residents experience that reputation from a distance. The gradual diffusion of quality dining into neighborhoods like Henderson, Summerlin, and the southwestern valley is changing what it means to live in greater Las Vegas. It is no longer necessary to travel to a casino to eat at a restaurant with genuine culinary ambition.

For Henderson specifically, the combination of population growth, strong household incomes across many of its ZIP codes, and the city's own emerging identity as a destination separate from Las Vegas creates favorable conditions for exactly the kind of restaurant that Hayworth represents. Chef-driven, independently owned, rooted in a specific culinary perspective, capable of becoming a neighborhood institution over time. Those restaurants are what turn a collection of good neighborhoods into a genuine city.

Alex Reznik chose Henderson for his debut restaurant. That choice reflects a reading of this market that I think is exactly right. The residents here are ready for what Hayworth is offering. They have been ready for a while. And now that Hayworth has opened and earned strong early attention, it is reasonable to expect that its success will encourage other talented chefs and operators to take a closer look at what Henderson has to offer as a home for serious food.

The 80-seat room at 1450 W. Horizon Ridge Pkwy. is a small space by most measures. But the conversation it is starting about Henderson as a dining destination is considerably larger than 80 seats. Keep an eye on what opens in this corridor and in the neighborhoods around it over the next few years. The foundations being laid right now are exactly the kind that serious restaurant scenes are built on.
