**Subject:** Why Zillow gets your value wrong

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Most homeowners check Zillow to see what their home is worth. It feels easy. But that number can be off by quite a bit.

Here's why. Zillow uses an algorithm. It pulls public data like square footage, lot size, and recent sales nearby. But it can't see inside your home. It doesn't know you upgraded the kitchen, added a patio, or have a brand new roof. It also doesn't account for the condition of the home or the specific street you live on.

In Las Vegas, values can vary a lot even within the same zip code. A home backing a park and a home backing a busy road might be 500 feet apart, but worth very different amounts.

The most accurate way to estimate your home's market value is a comparative market analysis, or CMA. It uses recent sales of similar homes in your area, adjusted for the things that make your home unique.

### EMAIL 21 — Day 281 | Category: Home Value & Equity
**Delay:** 281 days
**Subject:** Your home has three different values

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Did you know your home has three different "values" at any given time? They each mean something different, and mixing them up can cost you money.

**Assessed value** is what the county uses to calculate your property taxes. It's usually lower than what your home would actually sell for. Don't use this number when pricing your home.

**Appraised value** is what a licensed appraiser determines your home is worth. This comes into play when a buyer uses a mortgage to purchase. The bank wants to make sure they're not lending more than the home is worth.

**Market value** is what a buyer is actually willing to pay. This is the number that matters most when you're thinking about selling. It's driven by supply, demand, location, condition, and what similar homes have sold for recently.

**Subject:** What moves home values in Vegas

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Las Vegas home values don't move randomly. A few key things push them up or down.

**What drives values up:**
- Low inventory (fewer homes for sale means more competition)
- Population growth and job growth
- New businesses and infrastructure moving in
- Upgrades and renovations in your neighborhood

**What drives values down:**
- Too many homes on the market at once
- Foreclosures or short sales nearby
- Deferred maintenance in the neighborhood
- Major employers leaving the area

Las Vegas has a unique market because of tourism, entertainment, and the constant flow of people relocating here. That keeps demand strong in most areas.

**Subject:** The equity trap most sellers miss

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Your home might be worth $500,000. But that doesn't mean you'd walk away with $500,000 if you sold it.

There's a gap between your home's value and the cash you'd actually pocket. Here's what comes out:

- Your remaining mortgage balance
- Agent commissions
- Closing costs and title fees
- Any repairs or credits negotiated by the buyer
- Potential capital gains taxes

These costs can add up to 8-10% of the sale price. On a $500,000 home, that's $40,000 to $50,000.

**Subject:** Why your neighbor's sale matters

Hi #lead_first_name#,

When a home on your street sells, it affects your home's value. Here's how.

Buyers and appraisers use "comps," which are recent sales of similar homes nearby. These comps set the price range for your home.

If your neighbor's home sells for a strong price, it raises the bar for your home too. If it sells low, maybe due to poor condition or a rushed sale, it can pull your value down.

**Subject:** Gross equity vs. what you keep

Hi #lead_first_name#,

There are two equity numbers every homeowner should know.

**Gross equity** is your home's value minus what you owe on your mortgage. It's the big, exciting number.

**Net proceeds** is what you'd actually deposit in your bank account after selling. It subtracts commissions, closing costs, repairs, and any other fees.

The gap between these two numbers surprises most people. It's usually 8-10% of the sale price.
