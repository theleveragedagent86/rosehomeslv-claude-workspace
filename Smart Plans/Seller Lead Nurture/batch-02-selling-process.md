**Subject:** How selling a home works in Nevada

Hi #lead_first_name#,

If you've been thinking about selling, you might wonder what the process actually looks like. Here's a simple breakdown of how it works in Nevada, from start to finish.

**Step 1: Get your home ready.** This means small repairs, cleaning, and maybe some updates. Most sellers spend 1 to 3 weeks on this.

**Step 2: Price it right and list it.** Your agent helps you pick a price based on recent sales. Then photos get taken, and your home goes live on the market.

**Step 3: Showings and offers.** Buyers tour your home. When someone wants it, they send a written offer. You can accept, counter, or pass.

**Step 4: Under contract.** Once you agree on terms, the buyer does inspections and gets their loan finalized. This part usually takes 30 to 45 days.

**Step 5: Closing.** You sign the paperwork, hand over the keys, and get your money. In Nevada, a title company handles most of this.

Start to finish, most sellers should plan for about 60 to 90 days total. Some homes move faster. Some take a bit longer. It depends on your price, your area, and the market.

### EMAIL 22 — Day 295 | Category: Selling Process Education
**Delay:** 295 days
**Subject:** Why overpricing your home backfires

Hi #lead_first_name#,

One of the most common mistakes sellers make is pricing too high "just to see what happens." I get the thinking. But here's what usually happens instead.

Buyers skip over it. Today's buyers do their homework. They compare your home to others in the area. If your price is out of range, they don't make a lower offer. They just move on.

Your home sits on the market. The longer it sits, the more buyers wonder what's wrong with it. That's not the impression you want.

You end up dropping the price. And by then, the excitement of a new listing is gone. Price drops can actually signal desperation, even if that's not the case.

The best strategy? Price it right from the start. That's how you get the most attention, the most showings, and often the best offers.

I use recent sales data, neighborhood trends, and my experience in the Las Vegas market to help sellers land on the right number. Not too high. Not too low. Right where buyers are ready to act.

**Subject:** How to prep for a home inspection

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Once your home goes under contract, the buyer will usually schedule an inspection. Here's what to expect.

A licensed inspector walks through your entire home. They check the roof, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, foundation, and more. It takes 2 to 4 hours.

After that, the buyer gets a report. They might ask you to make repairs, give a credit, or they might be happy with everything.

Here's how to prepare before your home goes on the market:

- Make sure all lights and outlets work
- Replace old air filters
- Fix leaky faucets or running toilets
- Clear access to the attic, water heater, and electrical panel
- Address any obvious issues you've been putting off

The fewer surprises in the report, the smoother your sale goes.

### EMAIL 52 — Day 715 | Category: Selling Process Education
**Delay:** 715 days
**Subject:** What if the appraisal comes in low?

Hi #lead_first_name#,

When a buyer uses a loan to purchase your home, their lender orders an appraisal. This is a professional opinion of your home's value.

If the appraisal matches or exceeds the sale price, everything moves forward. But sometimes it comes in low. That means the lender won't approve the full loan amount.

When that happens, you have a few options:

- Lower the price to match the appraisal
- Ask the buyer to cover the gap with extra cash
- Meet somewhere in the middle
- Challenge the appraisal with better comparable sales

A low appraisal doesn't have to kill the deal. It just takes some problem solving.

This is one of those situations where having an experienced agent makes a real difference. If you ever want to talk through how this works, I'm here.

### EMAIL 62 — Day 855 | Category: Selling Process Education

**Subject:** How offers and counteroffers work

Hi #lead_first_name#,

When a buyer wants your home, they submit a written offer. That offer includes the price, their loan details, closing timeline, and any contingencies.

You can do one of three things: accept it, reject it, or send a counteroffer.

A counteroffer is your chance to adjust the terms. Maybe you want a higher price. Maybe you need a different closing date. Maybe you want fewer repairs.

Here's what's important to know: price isn't everything. The best offer might be the one with fewer contingencies, a stronger loan, or a faster close.

Once both sides agree, the contract is signed and the clock starts ticking on inspections, appraisals, and the loan process.

Good negotiation isn't about being tough. It's about knowing what matters most to you and making smart decisions.

**Subject:** What a title search protects you from

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Before your home sale closes, a title company runs a title search. This checks the public records to make sure there are no surprises.

They look for things like unpaid liens, ownership disputes, recording errors, or old judgments tied to the property.

If something shows up, it needs to be cleared before the sale can close. Most issues are fixable. But it's better to know early.

This is one of those behind the scenes steps that protects both you and the buyer.

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Real estate can get confusing when it comes to who works for whom. Here's the simple version.

A listing agent represents you, the seller. Their job is to get you the best price and terms.

### EMAIL 102 — Day 1415 | Category: Selling Process Education
**Delay:** 1415 days
**Subject:** Pre-listing vs. buyer inspections

Hi #lead_first_name#,

There are two types of inspections in a home sale. Most people only know about one.

A buyer inspection happens after you accept an offer. The buyer hires an inspector to check everything. If they find problems, they may ask for repairs or credits. This can slow things down or even kill a deal.

A pre-listing inspection is one you order before putting your home on the market. You find the issues first and fix them on your terms. No surprises. No last minute negotiations.

It's not required, but it can give you a real advantage. Want to know if it makes sense for your home? Just ask.
