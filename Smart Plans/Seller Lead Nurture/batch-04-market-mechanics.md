**Subject:** Supply and demand decide your price

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Here's something most homeowners don't think about when selling. Your home's price isn't just about upgrades or square footage. It's about supply and demand.

Real estate agents measure this with something called "months of inventory." It works like this. Take all the homes for sale right now. Then look at how many sold last month. Divide the first number by the second. That gives you the months of inventory.

If it's under 3 months, there are more buyers than homes. Sellers have the advantage. Prices tend to go up.

If it's over 6 months, there are more homes than buyers. That means buyers have more power, and prices can soften.

Between 3 and 6 months is pretty balanced.

Why does this matter for you? Because the number of homes on the market when you list has a direct effect on your sale price. Timing your listing around this number can mean thousands more in your pocket.

**Subject:** What "days on market" really means

Hi #lead_first_name#,

When a home sits on the market for a long time, buyers start to wonder what's wrong with it. That's why "days on market" matters so much.

Days on market (DOM) is simply how long a home has been listed for sale. A low number signals demand. A high number raises red flags for buyers.

Here's the thing. When DOM creeps up, buyers feel bolder. They make lower offers. They ask for more repairs. They negotiate harder.

But when DOM is low in your area, it means homes are moving fast. Sellers get stronger offers, sometimes over asking price.

This is why your listing strategy matters from day one. Pricing right, staging well, and launching with strong marketing helps keep your DOM low. And a low DOM protects your negotiating power.

**Subject:** How rates quietly change your value

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Most homeowners know that interest rates affect buyers. But many don't realize how much rates affect their own home's value.

Here's the simple version. When rates drop, buyers can afford more house with the same monthly payment. That pushes prices up. When rates rise, buyers can afford less. That puts downward pressure on prices.

Think about it this way. A buyer with a $2,500 monthly budget can afford a much bigger mortgage at 5% than at 7%. So when rates are lower, more buyers can compete for your home. That competition drives your price higher.

This doesn't mean you should try to time the market perfectly. But it does mean understanding where rates sit when you list can help you set realistic expectations and a smart pricing strategy.

### EMAIL 34 — Day 463 | Category: Market Mechanics

**Subject:** Buyer's market or seller's market?

Hi #lead_first_name#,

People throw around "buyer's market" and "seller's market" a lot. But what do they actually mean?

A seller's market means there are more buyers than homes. Sellers get multiple offers and stronger prices.

A buyer's market means there are more homes than buyers. Buyers have room to negotiate and take their time.

You can figure out which one you're in by looking at inventory levels, days on market, and how many homes are selling at or above asking price.

The good news? Homes sell in both markets. The strategy just changes. In a buyer's market, presentation and pricing matter even more. In a seller's market, timing your move is key.

**Subject:** How to read a CMA like a pro

Hi #lead_first_name#,

A CMA, or Comparative Market Analysis, is the report agents use to suggest a listing price. Knowing how to read one puts you in a stronger position.

The key things to look at: how recent are the comparable sales, how close are they to your home in size and condition, and what adjustments were made.

Not all CMAs are created equal. A good one uses truly comparable homes, not just nearby ones.
