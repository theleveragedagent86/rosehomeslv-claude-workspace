### EMAIL 26 — Day 351 | Category: Financial & Tax

**Subject:** The 2-out-of-5-year rule explained

Hey #lead_first_name#,

One of the most valuable tax benefits for homeowners is the primary residence exemption. But there's a specific rule you need to meet to qualify.

It's called the 2-out-of-5-year rule. Here's how it works.

To exclude your home sale profit from capital gains tax, you must have owned and lived in the home as your primary residence for at least two of the five years before the sale. Those two years don't have to be back to back. They just need to add up to 24 months within that five-year window.

**A few things to keep in mind:**

- You can use this exemption once every two years
- If you rented your home out for a while but moved back in, you may still qualify
- Partial exemptions may be available if you moved for work, health, or other qualifying reasons
- The two years of ownership and the two years of residency can overlap, but both must be met

This rule is one of the biggest tax breaks available to homeowners. If you're thinking about selling, it's smart to check where you stand with a tax advisor before you list.

### EMAIL 56 — Day 771 | Category: Financial & Tax
**Delay:** 771 days
**Subject:** What is a 1031 exchange, exactly?

Hi #lead_first_name#,

If you own an investment property, you may have heard of a 1031 exchange. Here's the basics.

A 1031 exchange lets you sell an investment property and reinvest the proceeds into another investment property while deferring your capital gains taxes. You don't avoid the tax forever, but you push it down the road.

There are strict rules. You have 45 days to identify a replacement property and 180 days to close on it. The new property must be of equal or greater value. And this only works for investment properties, not your primary home.

It's a powerful tool for building wealth through real estate, but the timeline is tight and mistakes can be costly.

**Subject:** Home upgrades can lower your tax bill

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Did you know that certain home improvements can reduce your capital gains tax when you sell?

Improvements that add value, extend the life of your home, or adapt it to new uses get added to your cost basis. That means they reduce your taxable profit.

Things like a new roof, kitchen remodel, added bathroom, or HVAC replacement all count. Regular maintenance like painting or fixing a leaky faucet does not.

Keep your receipts. They could save you real money at tax time.

**Subject:** Your home and estate planning basics

Hi #lead_first_name#,

If your home is your biggest asset, your heirs should know about something called a stepped-up basis.

When a home is inherited, the cost basis resets to the home's market value at the time of the owner's passing. That means years of appreciation could pass to your heirs with little or no capital gains tax.

This is a major benefit, but it requires proper estate planning. A trust, updated will, and a conversation with an estate attorney can make things much easier for your family.
