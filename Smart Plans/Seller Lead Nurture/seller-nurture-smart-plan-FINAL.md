### EMAIL 4 — Day 43 | Category: Market Mechanics
**Delay:** 43 days
**Subject:** Supply and demand decide your price

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Here's something most homeowners don't think about when selling. Your home's price isn't just about upgrades or square footage. It's about supply and demand.

Real estate agents measure this with something called "months of inventory." It works like this. Take all the homes for sale right now. Then look at how many sold last month. Divide the first number by the second. That gives you the months of inventory.

If it's under 3 months, there are more buyers than homes. Sellers have the advantage. Prices tend to go up.

If it's over 6 months, there are more homes than buyers. That means buyers have more power, and prices can soften.

Between 3 and 6 months is pretty balanced.

Why does this matter for you? Because the number of homes on the market when you list has a direct effect on your sale price. Timing your listing around this number can mean thousands more in your pocket.

**Subject:** Your home has three different values

Hi #lead_first_name#,

Did you know your home has three different "values" at any given time? They each mean something different, and mixing them up can cost you money.

**Assessed value** is what the county uses to calculate your property taxes. It's usually lower than what your home would actually sell for. Don't use this number when pricing your home.

**Appraised value** is what a licensed appraiser determines your home is worth. This comes into play when a buyer uses a mortgage to purchase. The bank wants to make sure they're not lending more than the home is worth.

**Market value** is what a buyer is actually willing to pay. This is the number that matters most when you're thinking about selling. It's driven by supply, demand, location, condition, and what similar homes have sold for recently.

### EMAIL 26 — Day 351 | Category: Financial & Tax

**Subject:** The 2-out-of-5-year rule explained

Hey #lead_first_name#,

One of the most valuable tax benefits for homeowners is the primary residence exemption. But there's a specific rule you need to meet to qualify.

It's called the 2-out-of-5-year rule. Here's how it works.

To exclude your home sale profit from capital gains tax, you must have owned and lived in the home as your primary residence for at least two of the five years before the sale. Those two years don't have to be back to back. They just need to add up to 24 months within that five-year window.

**A few things to keep in mind:**

- You can use this exemption once every two years
- If you rented your home out for a while but moved back in, you may still qualify
